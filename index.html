The Disease

What is White-Nose Syndrome?

White-Nose Syndrome (WNS) is a deadly fungal disease caused by Pseudogymnoascus destructans — a cold-loving fungus that thrives in the dark, humid caves where bats hibernate during winter.

The fungus attacks bats while they sleep, growing on their skin and causing them to wake up too often during hibernation. This burns through fat reserves they need to survive the winter, and most die before spring arrives.

The Fungus

Pseudogymnoascus destructans grows in cold, dark cave environments — perfect conditions for bat hibernation.

How It Spreads

The fungus spreads bat-to-bat and can survive on cave surfaces for years, even without a host.

The Impact

Infected bats wake repeatedly during hibernation, depleting fat stores and causing starvation before spring.

Where It Started

First detected in New York in 2006, WNS has since spread across most of North America.

Why Bats Matter

Bats and the Ecosystem

Bats play a critical role in maintaining healthy ecosystems across the United States. Losing them has consequences far beyond the caves they call home.

Pest Control

A single bat can eat up to 1,000 insects per hour — saving farmers billions in pesticide costs annually.

Pollination

Many plants — including agave, bananas, and mangoes — depend on bats for pollination.

Seed Dispersal

Fruit-eating bats spread seeds across wide areas, helping forests regenerate and grow.

Geographic Spread

Where in the US?

WNS was first detected in a cave near Albany, New York in 2006. Since then it has spread to over 40 states and parts of Canada, moving primarily through bat migration and human activity in caves.

Take Action

How You Can Help

WNS is a serious crisis, but there are real things everyday people can do to slow its spread and support bat conservation efforts across the country.

01

Stay Out of Caves

Avoid entering caves or mines where bats hibernate. Human activity can stress bats and spread the fungus between sites.

02

Decontaminate Gear

If you do explore caves, follow decontamination protocols for clothing and equipment to prevent spreading the fungus.

03

Build a Bat House

Installing a bat house provides safe roosting habitat for bats and helps support local bat populations near your home.

04

Support Research

Donate to organizations like Bat Conservation International that fund WNS research and conservation efforts.

05

Reduce Pesticides

Using fewer pesticides means more insects for bats to eat — helping already-stressed populations find food more easily.

06

Spread Awareness

Share what you know. The more people understand about WNS, the more support conservation efforts will receive.

Conservation Efforts

What Researchers Are Doing

Scientists and conservationists are actively working to fight WNS. Promising developments include a probiotic treatment using a naturally occurring bacterium that inhibits fungal growth, and trials of a vaccine that could be delivered to bats in the wild.

Federal agencies like the US Fish and Wildlife Service have invested millions into WNS response plans, and many states have active monitoring programs to track the disease's spread.
